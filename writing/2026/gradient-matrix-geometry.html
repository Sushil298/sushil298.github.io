Matrix Geometry of Gradient Descent

April 2026

In gradient descent, we define a loss function, compute its gradient, and move in the negative gradient direction. Each parameter has a derivative, so each parameter gets updated. This picture is useful, but it also leaves an important question.

The questions we can ask sound like this:

If the gradient already tells us how every parameter changes, then why do we need to talk about matrix norms, singular values, and condition numbers? Which matrix should we study? The weights? The loss? The input? And if, during SGD, even though we do not multiply the gradient matrix by some arbitrary vector, then why do we care about things like "this matrix acts strongly in some directions and weakly in others"?

These are exactly the right questions to ask.

The key is that there are two different things happening at once. The first is the actual optimization step, where we subtract the gradient. The second is the mathematical analysis of the structure of that gradient. SGD uses the gradient as an update object. Linear algebra helps us understand what kind of update object it really is.

This post is a slow, bottom-up story to connect those two views clearly.

A tiny one-layer neural network

Let us start with the smallest neural network where the gradient is naturally a matrix.

Take an input vector

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

and let a one-layer linear network produce a two-dimensional output using a weight matrix

\[ W = \begin{bmatrix} w_{11} & w_{12} \\ w_{21} & w_{22} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The network computes \( \hat y = Wx \). Suppose the target output is

\[ y = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

and we use squared error loss

\[ L(W) = \frac12 \|Wx - y\|_2^2. \]

Now everything is clear: \(x\) is the input, \(y\) is the target, \(W\) is the parameter matrix, and \(L(W)\) is a scalar function of that matrix. So the gradient with respect to \(W\) has the same shape as \(W\) — it is also a matrix.

The matrix we are studying is usually the layer weight matrix \(W\), and especially the gradient of the loss with respect to it, \(\nabla_W L\).

A concrete numerical example

Take

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad y = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad W = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

The prediction is \( \hat y = Wx = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} \), giving error \( e = \hat y - y = \begin{bmatrix}-2\\1\end{bmatrix} \) and loss \( L(W) = \tfrac{1}{2}(4+1) = \tfrac{5}{2} \).

For this squared loss, the gradient is

\[ \nabla_W L = (\hat y - y)x^\top = ex^\top = \begin{bmatrix}-2\\1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-2 & -4\\1 & 2\end{bmatrix}. \]

Let us call this gradient matrix \(G\). This is the object that SGD uses.

What SGD does with this matrix

Plain SGD updates the weights by subtracting a scaled copy of the gradient:

\[ W_{\text{new}} = W - \eta G. \]

So if \(\eta = 0.1\),

\[ W_{\text{new}} = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix} - 0.1 \begin{bmatrix}-2&-4\\1&2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1.2&0.4\\-0.1&0.8\end{bmatrix}. \]

At this point, a natural thought appears:

We can already see the entries of the gradient. They tell us exactly how each parameter changes. So why do we need anything more?

That is the heart of this blog, and it is the right place to slow down.

What the entries tell us — and what they don't

The entries of \(G\) tell us that \(w_{11}\) moves by \(-2\), \(w_{12}\) by \(-4\), and so on. That view is completely correct. But a matrix is not just a box of numbers — it can contain structure. Its entries may be highly dependent on one another. The whole matrix may really represent just one dominant pattern of change, or two balanced patterns, or one strong pattern plus many weak ones.

Notice that in our example \( G = ex^\top \) is an outer product of two vectors — a rank-1 matrix. The second row is \(-\tfrac12\) times the first. The second column is \(2\) times the first. Four entries, but only one true mode of variation.

The entries answer: how does each parameter change in this basis?
The singular values answer: how many true modes of variation are inside this update, and how strong are they?

Those are different questions.

A question we need to answer: "But SGD never computes \(Gv\)"

People often hear that to understand a matrix we study how it acts on vectors — what happens to \(Gv\) for different directions \(v\). Then they object:

But in SGD we never take some vector \(v\) and multiply it by the gradient matrix. We just scale the whole matrix by the learning rate and subtract it. So why are we talking about \(Gv\) at all?

This objection is absolutely correct. During optimization, SGD does not explicitly compute \(Gv\) for arbitrary \(v\). When linear algebra talks about \(Gv\), it is not describing what SGD literally does step by step. It is describing how we analyze the internal geometry of the matrix.

Why singular values reveal something entries cannot

The entries of a matrix depend on the coordinate system. If we rotate the basis, the entries change. But the singular values do not. So singular values reveal something intrinsic about the update that is not just an artifact of how we chose to write it down.

They tell us whether the update is spread across many modes, concentrated into one or two dominant modes, balanced across directions, or extremely skewed. That kind of information is hard to see from entries alone, especially in large matrices.

Matrix norms: different notions of size

The Frobenius norm treats all entries together:

\[ \|G\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} G_{ij}^2}. \]

For our matrix, \( \|G\|_F = \sqrt{4+16+1+4} = 5 \).

The spectral norm is the largest singular value:

\[ \|G\|_2 = \sigma_{\max}(G). \]

The Frobenius norm answers: how large is the whole update overall?
The spectral norm answers: what is the strongest single mode hidden inside the update?

Condition number: how uneven is the matrix?

The condition number is roughly

\[ \kappa(G) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}(G)}{\sigma_{\min}(G)}. \]

If the largest and smallest singular values are close, the matrix is balanced. If one is huge and another tiny, the matrix is highly skewed.

  • Norms measure size.
  • Singular values measure mode strengths.
  • Condition number measures unfairness across modes.

How this mathematically affects the SGD update

Suppose the gradient matrix has singular value decomposition \( G = U \Sigma V^\top \). Then

\[ \Delta W = -\eta G = -\eta U \Sigma V^\top. \]

Even though SGD does not explicitly inspect singular values, it inherits them exactly, up to scaling by the learning rate. If \(G\) has one dominant singular value, the SGD step also has one dominant singular value. If \(G\) is badly conditioned, the SGD step is badly conditioned too.

So singular values are not a side topic. They tell us the geometry of the update that SGD is about to make.

A concrete example where entries alone are misleading

Consider these two gradient matrices:

\[ G_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\\1 & 1\end{bmatrix} \qquad\text{and}\qquad G_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 1\\1 & -1\end{bmatrix}. \]

Every entry has magnitude \(1\), and both have the same Frobenius norm: \( \|G_1\|_F = \|G_2\|_F = 2 \). From entries and total norm alone, they look nearly identical.

But their singular values tell a completely different story.

For \(G_1\):

\[ \sigma_1 = 2,\qquad \sigma_2 = 0. \]

Rank 1. All action lives in a single dominant pattern.

For \(G_2\):

\[ \sigma_1 = \sqrt{2},\qquad \sigma_2 = \sqrt{2}. \]

Perfectly balanced across two modes.

Two gradient matrices with the same entry magnitudes and the same Frobenius norm, yet representing very different update geometries. SGD inherits that difference exactly.

Final takeaway

The entries of the gradient matrix tell us the update in our chosen coordinates.
The singular values tell us the intrinsic geometry of that update.

That is why both views matter. Norms tell us how large the update is. Singular values tell us how its strength is distributed across modes. Condition number tells us how unbalanced those modes are.
